- 1818 — Lutheran and Reformed congregations merged into United Protestant-Evangelical-Christian Church of the Palatinate
- 1946 — Became part of Rhineland-Palatinate

## Church Records — What Exists and Where

### Albisheim Lutheran/Protestant Parish
- **Parish established:** 1554
- **Records begin:** 1641 (with many gaps)
- **Parish not occupied 1688-1698** — administered from Gauersheim during this period
- **Parish coverage includes:** Immesheim, Fort-/Pfortmühle, Steinmühle, Sion (near Morschheim), Gauersheim, Rittersheim (from 1592)

### Important Note on "Reformed" vs. "Lutheran"
The Palatinate had a complex confessional history:
- **1560s** — Elector Frederick III promoted Reformed (Calvinist) faith
- **1583** — John Casimir restored Reformed faith
- **Late 1600s** — Reformed worship prohibited; many emigrated
- **1818** — All Palatine Lutheran and Reformed congregations **merged** into united Protestant congregations

The Schauss family was documented as Reformed (Calvinist), but records were kept in the Lutheran parish system. After 1818, all records are "Protestantisch" (united).

## Research Questions (Prioritized)

1. **~~What Schauss family members remained in Albisheim after 1736?~~** — **PARTIALLY ANSWERED (April 20, 2026):** The Trimble/Lindauer compilation (1987, based on Albisheim church records) documents only **5 children of Philipp Heinrich Schauss — ALL died young**: Johann (d. 1714), Anna Maria (d. 1719), Anna Barbara (d. 1727), Johann Conrad (d. 1731), Conrad (d. 1741). The "16 children" claim on Geni/Ancestry is **unverified and likely erroneous** — Trimble (the most detailed published source) lists only 5. His wife Anna Barbara _____ died March 4, 1729. The German church record transcription in the Trimble PDF notes he was "Zwei verheiratet" (married twice), opening the possibility of additional children from a second marriage. The Uhrig Ortsfamilienbuch (2009) is the definitive source to resolve this. **If no children from a second marriage survived, Philipp Heinrich's line ended with him in 1765 and the Schauss name in the Albisheim/Zellertal area today must derive from a different branch** (possibly descended from his brother Johannes Schauss, b. 1678, who married Maria Barbara Meyers in 1705 and had at least son Philipp Jacob, b. 1707).

2. **Where exactly is Büdingen (Lorraine)?** — Sources conflict: some say Lorraine, some say Westerwaldkreis. This is NOT Büdingen in Hesse. Identify the exact village to trace pre-Albisheim origins.

3. **What church records survive for Albisheim pre-1720?** — The parish began 1641 with gaps; Archion has from 1720. What exists for 1641-1720 (the period when the Schauss family arrived and first generation lived)?

4. **~~Are there Schauss graves in the Albisheim Protestant cemetery?~~** — **ANSWERED (April 19, 2026): Almost certainly not.** Germany's Ruhezeit (grave lease) system recycles plots every 20–30 years. The Albisheim Protestant churchyard at Kirchgasse 14 (adjacent to the Peterskirche) has been continuously active for centuries. The oldest protected headstones per the Denkmalliste Rheinland-Pfalz date from c. 1850 — elaborate Gründerzeit sandstone stelae (Schlossstein d. 1901, Hahn d. 1906). The last Schauss death in Albisheim was Philipp Heinrich in 1765, 85 years before the earliest surviving stone. No Schauss headstone appears in any monument registry, Find a Grave, BillionGraves, or CompGen Grabstein-Projekt. The family's physical burial evidence has been lost to routine cemetery management over 260 years. The record survives in the Kirchenbücher and Ortsfamilienbuch — paper, not stone. (Note: Eckweiler cemetery is frozen since 1979 abandonment and might theoretically have old stones, but the Schauss ancestors there predate the 1628 era of surviving headstones.)

5. **What does the Albisheim 1100th Anniversary Book contain about the Schauss family?** — Referenced in genealogical sources. Locate and examine.

## Notes and Observations

### The Reformed (Calvinist) Question
The user identifies the Schauss family as Reformed/Calvinist. However, the Albisheim parish is documented as **Lutheran** (established 1554). Possible explanations:
1. The family may have been Reformed but recorded in Lutheran parish books (common in mixed areas)
2. After the 1818 merger, all records became "Protestant" — earlier distinctions may be blurred in sources
3. The second marriage of Johann Conrad Sr. in Göllheim (1723) may indicate ties to a Reformed congregation there
4. Further research into the specific confessional history of the Albisheim parish is needed

### The Miller Connection
Johann Adam Schauss was a millwright/miller. The PRFK maintains a **Müllerdatenbank** (Miller Database) — this is a high-priority resource for finding additional records about him and possibly other Schauss family members in the milling trade.

### Potential for DNA Research
With extensive Schauss/Shouse descendants in North America and potentially remaining family in Germany, DNA testing could:
- Confirm connections between American and German branches
- Identify living German-side descendants
- Break through the Büdingen/Eckweiler brick wall

#### The "Schauß Mühle" (Schauss Mill) in Roxheim — Major Historic Site

Roxheim, Bad Kreuznach has an officially recognized settlement called **Schauß Mühle** — a Schauss-named mill on the Katzenbach creek, north of the Nahe river. It is one of two outlying homesteads (Wohnplätze) belonging to Roxheim, alongside Rollars Mühle. The site is significant enough to:
- Be listed as an **official Wohnplatz** (residential settlement) in Wikipedia and German administrative records
- Have its own **weather page** on WetterOnline and wetter-deutschland.com

- Appear on **OpenStreetMap** as a named location

**Why this is highly significant for the Schauss family:**

- The Eckweiler Schauss family is from the Bad Kreuznach district — this exact area
- **Three Schauss families still live in Roxheim today** (Dirk, Annette, and one other)

- Roxheim was in the **Electorate of the Palatinate, Oberamt of Kreuznach** — same political entity as Eckweiler
- The name appears in historical records for Roxheim as a recognized place name, not just a building

**ECKWEILER — The Abandoned Village (1979)**
Eckweiler, the earliest Schauss homeland, was **abandoned in 1979** when NATO expanded the Pferdsfeld airbase for F-4 Phantom jets. 350 residents were resettled to the Leinenborn area east of Bad Sobernheim. The village was demolished in 1981-82.
- **Only the church and cemetery survive** — the late-Gothic Heiligkreuzkirche (Holy Cross Church, c. 1500) is monument-protected
- **The cemetery still exists** but no new graves since abandonment. However, the earliest Schauss ancestors (Hanß d. unknown, Melchior d. 1628) predate the era of surviving headstones; any stones present likely date from the 19th–20th century
- **Visitable today:** The Freundeskreis Eckweilerer Kirche opens the church May-October, first Sunday each month, 2-5 PM
- **Hiking route:** "Vitaltour Um die Wüstung" — ~5km walk from Bad Sobernheim through the former village site
